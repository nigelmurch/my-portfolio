Why I Became a UX Researcher — and Why I’ll Never Stop

 

I’m a UX researcher and designer shaped by hardship, hope, and a relentless belief in people.

 

My journey into UX didn’t begin in a classroom. It began in the paddocks of Southland, in meatworks and firelines, in hospital wards and Work and Income offices. It began in the quiet moments of feeling invisible — and in the louder ones, where I refused to be.

 

I grew up in the 1980s obsessed with computers — fascinated by how they connected people to new worlds. But school was brutal. I left in Year 10, not because I wanted to, but because I had to. What followed was a life of hard labour, injury, and misdiagnosis. For ten years, I lived under the shadow of a condition I didn’t have, while the real one — Functional Neurological Disorder — went undetected. I felt erased.

 

But in the darkest moments, I found light in unexpected places. In video games, I found freedom. In storytelling, I found meaning. And in my daughter Tammi, I found purpose. When she passed away in 2021, I made a promise at her bedside: I will study again. I will make this life count.

 

That promise led me to Media Design School, where I began to shape a new path — one rooted in empathy, equity, and design. Today, I’m a UX specialist who asks meaningful questions, listens deeply, and co-creates experiences that support people in real, tangible ways.

 

My work is human-centred and purpose-driven. I believe people are the experts of their own lives, and my role is to listen, translate, and connect — turning lived experience into actionable insight. I’ve worked alongside Māori communities, eco-conscious groups, and rangatahi navigating stigma in healthcare. These collaborations have taught me that design has the most power where silence, shame, or systems have failed.

 

One project I’m proud of is Kōtuitui Toi HealthCare, a sexual health initiative for young people in Aotearoa. Through inclusive research and co-design, we created a mobile service that met rangatahi where they were — with testing kits, soundproof booths, and vibrant, youth-led visuals. It wasn’t just design. It was dignity.

 

Another project explored how storytelling could help communities embrace wild and invasive species as sustainable food sources — blending environmental care with cultural values and behavioural insight.
