Te Pou Theatre Project

About Te Pou Theatre's Project



Project Overview

Te Pou Theatre approached us with a powerful challenge: to strengthen their presence as the heart of performing arts in West Auckland. We saw the opportunity to redesign their website as a way to amplify their kaupapa, celebrate te ao Māori, and connect more deeply with the communities they serve.

Te Pou is more than a theatre – it’s a living, breathing space for te reo Māori, community storytelling, and indigenous creativity. Our job wasn’t just to make a website; it was to reflect that mana (prestige, authority) online in a way that was functional, respectful, and visually powerful.

We took on the role of digital kaitiaki (guardians), ensuring the online presence matched the spirit and identity of Te Pou Theatre. This meant respecting tikanga (customs), enhancing accessibility, and making sure the site would serve both the local community and wider audiences.



My Role

As a UX researcher and designer, I led the user research. I wanted every design decision to be grounded in real needs – from kaimahi (staff), to creatives, to regular attendees. I studied competing theatre websites and explored what made Te Pou’s kaupapa (purpose) stand out.

I created low-fidelity wireframes, guided content hierarchy, and helped shape the tone and voice of the website. It needed to feel warm and grounded in te ao Māori, yet still usable for a broad audience.

I worked closely with our team — especially with Noah, our interactive designer. His Māori knowledge far surpassed my own, and he acted as our cultural bridge throughout the project.



The Design Process

We started with mood boards and competitor research to understand how other indigenous and creative organisations express themselves digitally. A key insight emerged: many theatre websites feel cold or overly ticket-focused. But Te Pou’s strength is in its people and their stories — that needed to shine.

We developed core user personas and journey maps for three audience groups:

  • Whānau and community members wanting to know what’s on and how to attend
  • Performers and artists looking for opportunities, rehearsal space, or funding pathways
  • Organisational partners and funders seeking clarity on Te Pou’s impact and values

We co-designed key pages — homepage, what’s on, kaupapa, and booking info — all tested against the question: “Does this honour Te Pou’s wairua?”



Highlights & Wins

  • Cultural integrity upheld:
    Our design respected tikanga Māori, using authentic colour, imagery, and type choices.
  • Clear structure, warm tone:
    balanced intuitive navigation with a welcoming, mana-enhancing voice.
  • Accessibility-first:
    contrast, font choices, and alt text were part of our design system from the beginning.


What I’d Improve

  • More whānau testing:
    didn’t have time for a full usability round with the community — a priority for future iterations.
  • Dynamic content:
    love to integrate behind-the-scenes videos or audio stories to bring the site to life.
  • Bilingual UX:
    used some te reo Māori, but a fuller dual-language experience would better reflect our goals of language revitalisation.


Future Potential

This project is just the beginning. Te Pou’s site could grow into a digital marae — a space for gathering, learning, and connection. Ideas for future development include:

  • A wānanga space for artists and writers
  • A community noticeboard
  • Backend tools for kaimahi to manage shows, bookings, and events more easily


Final Thoughts

This project meant a lot to me personally. As someone reconnecting with their whakapapa and cultural identity, being trusted to work on a kaupapa Māori project like this was both a privilege and a responsibility.

It reminded me that design isn’t just about screens and pixels — it’s about people, stories, and legacy. Te Pou Theatre has the potential to become a digital beacon for Māori performing arts. I’m proud of the foundation we laid — and excited for what’s ahead.
