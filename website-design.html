Group Projects

Open Door Project

What Went Right

  1. The core idea of accessibility and inclusion through the metaphor of a "door" was strong, universal, and relatable.
  2. Our team operated with a genuine heart. Everyone brought something unique to the table — whether that was visual design, research, storytelling, or cultural insight.
  3. The design process moved well through paper prototypes, lo-fi sketches, and found a more developed visual language.

What Went Wrong

  1. The visuals didn’t fully match the emotional weight of the story we were trying to tell.
  2. Some elements of the project were rushed. The refinement stage felt squeezed.
  3. We didn’t conduct enough real-world or community-based testing to validate our design decisions.
  4. There were inconsistencies or vagueness around some messaging, including tone and hierarchy.
  5. Remote teamwork introduced communication gaps that impacted alignment and clarity.

Future Possibilities

  1. Immersive installations using motion, sound, or AR posters to deepen user engagement.
  2. Co-designing with communities so each story evolves with authentic representation.
  3. Turning it into a digital platform that supports education, resources, and lived-experience advocacy.
  4. Broader accessibility themes could tie into mental health, justice, public services or housing equity.

Final Thoughts

The Open Door Project could grow into a truly transformative piece of design activism. Our team laid a strong foundation for future work rooted in community engagement and inclusive design.

Whetū Mārama

What Went Right

  1. The concept of Whetū Mārama as a guiding light was powerful and resonated with themes of whakapapa, resilience, and identity.
  2. We anchored our design in te ao Māori values, which gave the work a strong cultural foundation and sense of purpose.
  3. The visual direction — inspired by retro-futurism — was bold, unique, and gave the project a distinct personality.
  4. Our team collaborated well across disciplines, blending design, storytelling, and research into a cohesive whole.

What Went Wrong

  1. Some of the symbolic and cultural references may not have been clearly communicated to broader audiences unfamiliar with the kaupapa.
  2. The audio and animation elements were ambitious, and some were only partially realised due to time constraints.
  3. There were moments where we drifted too far into aesthetics and needed to pull back to reinforce clarity of message.
  4. User testing was limited, especially with intergenerational or kaumātua voices that could have strengthened the direction.

Future Possibilities

  1. Expanding Whetū Mārama into a cross-platform storyworld, including animation, interactive storytelling, or game-based learning tools.
  2. Deeper partnerships with iwi or cultural advisors to co-create future content and ensure rich authenticity.
  3. Creating a physical installation or night-time light experience where stories are projected onto real spaces.
  4. Translating the narrative into education modules to be used in kura kaupapa and mainstream schools alike.

Final Thoughts

Whetū Mārama brought together creativity, cultural respect, and futuristic vision in a way that felt truly meaningful. While the project isn’t perfect, it has the spark of something greater — a potential taonga for future storytelling in Aotearoa.
