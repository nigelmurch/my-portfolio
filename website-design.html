Why I Am Passionate About Flavours by Papatūānuku

Flavours by Papatūānuku is more than just a project.

It is a reflection of who I am, what I value, and the kind of future I want to help create. As someone with a deep connection to both the natural world and the people of Aotearoa, I feel a personal and cultural responsibility to protect and celebrate our environment. This project combines my passions for sustainability, design, storytelling, and social change in a way that feels not only urgent but deeply meaningful. If we want to change the world, then change starts at home.

At its heart, Flavours by Papatūānuku is about restoring balance and reimagining our relationship with food—not as passive consumers, but as active kaitiaki (guardians) of the whenua (land). Invasive species threaten our ecosystems and biodiversity—yet, ironically, they can also provide nutritious and delicious solutions if approached creatively. Yes, you heard that right!

By encouraging people to incorporate wild and invasive species into their diets, this project transforms an environmental problem into a cultural opportunity for reconnection, resilience, and innovation.

My passion stems from a drive to challenge the status quo, flip the narrative around food fear and waste, and inspire others to embrace unfamiliar flavours with curiosity. I believe food is one of the most powerful tools to bring people together, spark conversation, and drive change. Every recipe, story, and interaction through Flavours by Papatūānuku is designed to educate, empower, and excite people about the possibility of living more lightly and consciously on the land—while also fostering financial stability and reducing waste. In some cases, this bounty can be given to those suffering from poverty.

What fuels me is seeing eco-conscious individuals—from whānau to foragers to chefs—start to shift their thinking and embrace new ways of eating that honor both tradition and transformation. My design background enables me to translate this vision into compelling experiences—but it’s the kaupapa that gives it life.

This project is a wero (challenge) to all of us: to think critically about the impact of our choices, to celebrate the gifts of Papatūānuku, and to imagine a future where sustainability isn’t a compromise—it’s a joy.

Just A Thought

We often hear about rabbits, possums, wallabies, and other invasive species causing harm to Aotearoa’s delicate ecosystems. Yet the solution of aerial 1080 drops can be cruel, imprecise, and toxic. It saturates the land, contaminates waterways, and leads to agonizing deaths of animals—many of which become hazardous to other species, including humans. What if, instead, we reclaimed this problem as a resource? What if we cooked, cured, and celebrated these animals in a way that helped heal the land?
